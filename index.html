Applied Machine Learning and AI· Psychiatric Services Research

Hello! I'm a psychiatry resident in Stanford's Humanities and Social Medicine Research Track, the track's inaugural resident. My research builds and validates machine learning models that identify and engage patients with untreated psychiatric and substance use disorders using multimodal data, work supported by generous private and corporate funding and mentored by Keith Humphreys and Jonathan Chen. I'm technical lead on a supervised ML model for EHR-based SUD detection accepted at Nature Mental Health, and I write on psychiatric services and health policy alongside it, including recent papers in JAMA Health Forum on private equity-owned residential treatment facilities and JAMA Network Open on the economics of telemedicine "house calls." One goal of this work is to move these models out of publications and into clinical workflows, where they can flag patients that slip through the cracks of the mental healthcare system.

Previously I worked in Robert Rosenheck's lab at Yale, studying quality of life and treatment-seeking among people with substance use disorders rooted in legal, partially legal, and illegal substances. Broadly, I'm interested in why people with a psychiatric or substance use disorder do or don't seek care, and how financing and policy shape that decision. In practice, I've studied this using national survey data on quality of life and treatment history, Medicare and commercial claims, and the Open Payments database tracking industry payments to psychiatrists. For one project pairing psychiatric services research with health economics, on Medicaid costs and outcomes in outpatient telepsychiatry, see here.

I earned my M.D. and M.B.A. at Yale, supported by a Dean's Merit Scholarship, and studied quantitative methods in operations research alongside medicine.

Before medical school, as an undergraduate at the University of Chicago, I studied neuroscience with Peggy Mason. There, I ran experiments testing whether rats would free a trapped cagemate under various stressors and anxiolytics, and found that the presence of a passive bystander made rats less likely to help, the same bystander effect long documented in humans. That work was published in Science Advances and earned me one of two Honors degrees conferred in Neuroscience that year.
